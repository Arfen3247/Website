2025 Reading

  • 'The Picture of Dorian Gray' — Oscar Wilde

    As you have seen, the year started out pretty slow! I read The Picture of Dorian Gray by Oscar Wilde across the first few months. Sadly, I found it much less blatantly homosexual provocative than I had hoped for been warned: it was famously used as evidence when he was put on trial for daring to be a homosexual in 1890's Dublin. I have since learned that there are three versions of this book: the original manuscript he sent to the magazine Lippincott's, the heavily-edited edition they published in 1890, and the further censored text Wilde eventually published as a book the next year. The third, most watered-down version is what survives as the most common one today, and is the one on my shelf. It was the previous, far more provocative versions which were used as evidence in his trial. I do recommend the book that I read, even as a thought exercise on hedonism and deceit, though I wish to read his original eventually!

  • 'Meditations' — Marcus Aurelius

    After this I finally got around to reading `Meditations' by Marcus Aurelius, which has been on my reading list since before I started university! Oh well, better late than never. If you haven't heard of it, the book is a collection of private journals kept by the Roman Emperor as he was away on a war campaign. He would never return home, dying of a contagious disease, presumably the Antonine Plague. The jornals were not written with the aim to be published, but were personal exercises in Stoicism. He has since become the face of this philosophy, and his book its bible. That is what makes the words so meaningful, I find. They were not written by some self-help coach attempting to separate you from your money, but by a man striving to lead a virtuous life despite having been thrust into the most powerful position on the continent — "Take care not to be Caesarified, or dyed in purple ... Strive hard to remain the same man that philosophy wished to make you.", as he wrote (to himself!) in Book 6.

  • 'Remembrance of Earth's Past' — Cixin Liu

    Sometime in May, I was wandering a bookshop and came across 'The Three Body Problem' by Cixin Liu, the first of his trilogy 'Remembrance of Earth's Past'. I had seen the first Netflix adaptation of the first part of the story and strongly enjoyed it, so I was excited to read the book. It did not disappoint! I won't talk much about the plot, all you need to know is that mankind comes into contact with a far more advanced civilisation from Alpha Centauri, which in the book is a chaotic trinary star system. This civilisation (the Trisolarians) has launched an invasion fleet on a 400 year journey to conquer Earth, and we follow a Chinese physicist as the world freaks out at this news.
    One thing I have huge respect for is how it deals with describing aliens. Most popular portrayals echo the antiquated belief that we were made in God's image: any intelligent species must look humanoid. Liu avoids this by giving essentially zero physical description of the aliens, apart from two pieces of information. First, they are able to dehydrate their bodies (reminiscent of tardigrades) to survive extinction in the times where their planet is flung to far from, or comes too close to, one of their suns. Second, their thoughts show up as flashes of light on their skin. Thus, they have no distinction between 'to think' and 'to say', and no concept of deceit or lies. This is one way of avoiding the awkward question of 'Why did the villain reveal his plan prematurely?', by designing the aliens to not realise that communication with the Earth was a strategic mistake.
    Anyway, I thoroughly enjoyed the book, and in fact then read the second and third in quick succession. Each book starts in the 'present-day' (somewhere in the 2010's) and follows a separate character through the crisis. (The Netflix adaptation seems to cover the events chronologically, and includes the start of the second and third books.) The second book gets a bit slow in the middle, but the last 100-150 pages contain one of the most entertaining sequences of events I have ever read. I would recommend the series solely for those chapters. The book does not finish on a cliff-hanger per se, but resolves the main conflict and leaves many questions unanswered / open to interpretation, to the extent that I believe it possible to not read the final book.

    The third book is a slight disappointment by comparision. It tries to tie together such a huge story, that it falls into a common trap of such The story is still about as good, but the story-telling to science-fiction ratio becomes a little lopsided, (a symptom of having a huge conflict set in the future, we must introduce an unholy tree of technology) and as I said, That being said, the ending still made me cry, the technology introduced has given me countless hours of thought and conversation, and I still thoroughly enjoyed.

    As much as I did enjoy that series, it did suffer from one common symptom of such sci-fi epics: you cannot relate to the people (with the exception of Liu Ji of book 2). Every character lives and dies almost exactly the same as they were introduced. They do not have character arcs, only a sequence of events they live through. It is much harder to relate to a character who is stagnant and doesn't seem to work on their flaws; who is so shallow that they appear two-dimensional. This left a very dry taste in my mouth, so to speak, and with but amonth left until my thesis was due, I craved to read something where I could relate to a fucking character! Luckily for me, I had some of George Orwell's work sitting unread on my shelf.

  • 'Keep the Aspidistra Flying', 'A Clergyman's Daughter' — George Orwell

    I had read both '1984' and 'Animal Farm' a few years ago (on the recommendation of a friend at the time, for which I will always be grateful), and I had bought them as a set of 6 of Orwell's books. So, craving a story as much about a character as the unfair world they inhabit, this is where I turned. Over the course of a couple of weeks, I read both 'Keep the Aspidistra Flying' and 'A Clergyman's Daughter'. These are very similar books, following flawed characters as they navigate life on the brink of homelessness in an even-more-flawed London. They are very well-written, presumably because such was Orwell's own life at the time. The two situations were slightly different: the first character got there through his own self-destructive tendencies, the second through sheer misfortune (of being poor and a woman). These really hit the spot... I should read more Orwell in the future...

  • 'Hitch-22' — Chritopher Hitchens

    I am currently reading 'Hitch-22', the autobiography of Christopher Hitchens. I know Hitchens mainly as one of the 'Four Horsemen' of New Atheism, along with Dawkins, Dennett, and Harris. This is actually not too relevant to the autobiography, so I will leave further comments on that in the later paragraphs.

    Now back to how I discovered Hitchens. As do many young teenagers interested in physics, I slowly lost my faith. But in rural Ireland, where you were still judged, jokingly or not, for being the wrong flavour of Christian, I felt I had no-one to talk to about it. For example, it was not unheard of for the elderly Catholic relatives of someone marrying a Protestant to boycott the wedding. So, I turned to the Internet, where YouTube hosted a great many videos which picked apart religious arguments*. If you are interested, here are some creators: Alex O'Connor (I think he went by Cosmic Skeptic at the time), Genetically Modified Skeptic, The Atheist Experience (only up to the time Matt Dillahunty left), Professor Stick. It wasn't too long then until I can across the four great writers and orators of the New Atheism movement, of which Hitchens is probably the most well-known. For better or for worse, my dislike of organised religion (part of a wider hatred of any undemocratic concentration of power which is a net negative on society: Fuck the Church, Eat Tax the Rich, Melt Down The Crown and so forth) was largely shaped by Hitchens, and the views expressed so wonderfully in his magnum opus 'God is not Great', which I got around to reading last summer.

    *Unfortunate tangent time. Many such channels thrived by making viral moments (duh). The more aggregious (not those listed above) would find clips making poor arguments to defend some claim, and debunk these instead of a more sound argument. Effectively, finding a strawman instead of making one. This is fairly easy, and mostly harmless, to do for religious beliefs (which, to my knowledge, have precisely zero sound defence) and many conspiracy theories (some of which are defensible, others less so). The problem is that some did this to other (mostly liberal) political beliefs — there was a seemingly bottomless pit of 'modern feminists' and 'social justice warriors', or rather strawmen of these, on which they preyed. For someone like me, (young, politically uneducated, impressionable), such content unfortunately presented a very one-sided view of the world: the right were merely 'protecting traditional values', while the left were 'uneducated extremists'... I shudder at the thought of who I could have been indoctrinated into becoming. Don't worry, I had seen the light by the time I went to university (though I am still embarrasingly uneducated in many political matters).
